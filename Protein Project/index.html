Protein Interactions Calculator (PIC) is a server which recognizes various kinds of interactions; such as disulphide bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic interactions, hydrogen bonds, aromatic- aromatic interactions, aromatic-sulphur interactions and cation - π interactions within a protein or between proteins in a complex.

It also determines the accessible surface area as well as the distance of a residue from the surface of the protein. The input should be in the Protein data bank(.pdb) format. Interactions are calculated based on empirical or semi-empirical set of rules.

A disulfide bond is a covalent bond, usually derived by the coupling of two thiol groups. The linkage is also called an SS-bond or disulfide bridge. The overall connectivity is therefore C-S-S-C.

The other sulfur-containing amino acid, methionine, cannot form disulfide bonds. The prototype of a protein disulfide bond is the two-amino-acid peptide, cystine, which is composed of two cysteine amino acids joined by a disulfide bond Pairs of cysteines (sulphur atoms) within 2.2Å are considered as disulphide bridges.

Hydrophobic interactions are the most important non-covalent forces that will cause the linear polypeptide to fold into a compact structure. The hydrophobic (literally, 'water-hating') effect is named for the tendency of certain oil-like substances to avoid contact with water (proverbially, 'oil and water don't mix').

It is generally understood to be the driving force responsible for the folding of proteins. The aggregation of the hydrophobic surfaces gives the tightly packed core of a protein. The following residues are considered to participate in interactions if they fall within 5Å range: ALA, VAL, LEU, ILE, MET, PHE, TRP, PRO, TYR.

Cation-pi interaction is a non-covalent molecular interaction between phase of an electron rich system (e.g. benzene, ethylene and acetylene) and adjacent cation (e.g. Na+ and Li+).

The arginine side chain can participate in multiple interactions, providing a mechanism for inter-protein specificity. The interaction is an example for non covalent bonding between monopole and quadrupole. The following residues are considered to participate in interactions if they fall within 6Å range: ARG, LYS, PHE, TRP, TYR.

Ionic Interaction




Enter the interaction cut-off value Default(4.5A)

At any pH, protein have charged group that may participate in binding them to each other or two other type of molecules. Ionic interactions are often thought of as "strong" by virtue of their strength in vacuum, but are relatively weak in an aqueous environment.

For a protein molecule, the majority of charged groups are at the surface of the folded protein, where they are solvated and neutralized by counter ions of the salts present in the solution. Occasionally, there are ion pairs buried in the protein interior that can contribute to stabilization of folding because the local dielectric constant D is much lower in the hydrophobic interior of the protein.

Hydrophilicity also comes from the Greek word Hydro (water) and Philicity (friendship) it refers to a physical property of a material that can transiently bond with water (H2O) through hydrogen bonding. Furthermore it allows the liquid to enter the pores of a material and totally wet it.

Substances that bond to water are considered hydrophilic or “water loving” When water is placed on these substances they form a small contact angle. The evaluation of hydrophilicity is made through water contact angle measurements. A water droplet would occupy as long surface of the hydrophilic material as possible.

So the water contact angle will be significantly low. This type of interaction involves hydrophilic amino acids interacting with each other. Example for hydrophilic amino acids interacting are arginine, aspargine, aspartate, glutamine, histidine, lysine, serine, threonine, glutamate.

Aromatic-Aromatic Interaction




Enter the interaction cut-off value Default(4.5A)

This type of interaction occurs between two aromatic amino acids, e.g.: The interaction involving phenyl alanine and tryptophan. Hydrogen atoms present in aromatic rings takes part in this interaction. The electrons of the aromatic rings are localised on both sides of the flat ring, so that there is a small partial negative charge on the face, and a small partial positive charge on the hydrogen on the edge, which gives the possibility of electrostatic interactions.

This type of interaction is present between side chains of 50-60% of the aromatic amino acids. Side chain aromatic rings tend to stack with edge to face at an angle of 60-90° in about 2 out of 3 interactions; when they stack face to face the rings are offset so that the partial charges may interact.

The binding energy is of the order of 1-2 kcal mol-1. The contribution of free energy to protein stability is estimated to be relatively small and of the order of 0.6- 1.3 kcal mol-1.

Aromatic-Sulphur Interaction




Enter the interaction cut-off value Default(4.5A)

This type of interaction occurs between an aromatic amino acid and sulphur containing amino acid, e.g.: The interaction involving phenyl alanine and cystine. Hydrogen atoms of aromatic ring and sulphur atoms of cystine take part in this interaction.

The sulphur atom can interact favourably with an aromatic ring via an SK interaction and they are commonly observed at the hydrophobic cores of protein and may contribute towards protein stability.

Hydrogen bonding is the covalent bond formed between oxygen atom and hydrogen atom of amide groups. It is one of the difficult interactions to be identified due to the presence of main chain and side chain hydrogen atoms. Hydrogen bonds provide most of the directional interactions that underpin protein folding, protein structure and molecular recognition.

The core of most protein structures is composed of secondary structures such as α helix and β sheet. This satisfies the hydrogenbonding potential between main chain carbonyl oxygen and amide nitrogen buried in the hydrophobic core of the protein
